The Ruy Lopez

Key Moves

The Ruy Lopez is characterized by the moves:

1. e4 e5
2. Nf3 Nc6
3. Bb5

Main Ideas

White aims to pressure the knight on c6 which defends the e5 pawn. The bishop also prepares castling. After 3...a6, White has several options:

  • Exchange Variation: 4. Bxc6, doubling Black's pawns
  • Closed Variation: 4. Ba4, retreating but maintaining the pin
  • Open Variation: Leads to open positions after d4

Step-by-Step Development

  1. White establishes presence in the center with 1.e4
  2. Black responds symmetrically with 1...e5
  3. White develops knight to f3, attacking e5
  4. Black defends with knight to c6
  5. White pins the knight with bishop to b5
  6. Common continuation: 3...a6 4.Ba4 Nf6 5.0-0

Sicilian Defense

How to Play
History

Key Moves

The Sicilian Defense begins with:

1. e4 c5

Main Ideas

Black immediately counters White's center control with a flank pawn. This creates asymmetrical positions and fights for the d4 square. The main variations include:

  • Najdorf Variation: After 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6
  • Dragon Variation: Features fianchettoing the bishop to g7
  • Classical Variation: With ...d6 and ...Nc6 development

Step-by-Step Development

  1. White plays 1.e4, claiming central space
  2. Black responds asymmetrically with 1...c5
  3. White typically plays 2.Nf3, preparing to open the center
  4. Black has several options including 2...d6, 2...Nc6, or 2...e6
  5. White often continues with 3.d4, challenging the center
  6. After exchanges, complex imbalanced positions arise

History of the Sicilian Defense

This opening gets its name from an Italian chess manuscript from the late 16th century. However, it didn't gain significant popularity until the mid-20th century.

The opening saw a surge in popularity in the 1950s and 1960s when players like Bobby Fischer and Mikhail Tal used it with great success. It challenged the notion that 1...e5 was the only strong response to 1.e4.

Today, the Sicilian is the most common response to 1.e4 at the highest levels, known for creating sharp, unbalanced positions that offer Black good winning chances.

Notable Players

The Sicilian has been employed by numerous world champions and top players including Garry Kasparov, Bobby Fischer, and Viswanathan Anand.

Queen's Gambit

How to Play
History
Video

Key Moves

The Queen's Gambit starts with:

1. d4 d5
2. c4

Main Ideas

White offers a pawn sacrifice to gain control of the center. The main variations are:

  • Queen's Gambit Accepted: 2...dxc4, taking the offered pawn
  • Queen's Gambit Declined: 2...e6, reinforcing the d5 pawn
  • Slav Defense: 2...c6, supporting d5 with another pawn

Step-by-Step Development

  1. White starts with 1.d4, controlling the center
  2. Black responds with 1...d5, also claiming central space
  3. White plays 2.c4, attacking Black's d5 pawn
  4. If Black accepts: 2...dxc4, White develops pieces to recapture later
  5. If Black declines: 2...e6, White typically continues with 3.Nc3
  6. White aims for a strong pawn center and piece development

The Queen's Gambit is considered a positional opening, often leading to strategic maneuvering rather than immediate tactical skirmishes.

Key Moves

The King's Indian Defense begins with:

1. d4 Nf6
2. c4 g6
3. Nc3 Bg7
4. e4 d6

Main Ideas

Black allows White to build a strong center with pawns, planning to counterattack it later. Main variations include:

  • Classical Variation: With 5.Nf3 0-0 6.Be2
  • SÃ¤misch Variation: With 5.f3, emphasizing central control
  • Four Pawns Attack: With 5.f4, maximizing space advantage

Step-by-Step Development

  1. White begins with 1.d4, claiming the center
  2. Black plays 1...Nf6, developing a piece rather than a pawn
  3. White expands with 2.c4, and Black begins the fianchetto with 2...g6
  4. After 3.Nc3 Bg7, Black has completed the fianchetto
  5. White usually plays 4.e4, creating a strong pawn center
  6. Black continues with modest development, preparing for a later counterattack

History of the King's Indian Defense

While elements of this defense appeared in games from the 19th century, the King's Indian as a coherent system was developed in the 1920s and 1930s.

The opening was popularized by players like Bronstein and Boleslavsky in the 1940s. It became highly respected after Bobby Fischer adopted it with great success in the 1960s.

The King's Indian represents the hypermodern school of chess thought, which challenged classical dogma about controlling the center with pawns from the beginning of the game.

Notable Champions

The opening has been championed by aggressive players including Garry Kasparov, Bobby Fischer, and Mikhail Tal, who appreciated its dynamic counterattacking possibilities.
